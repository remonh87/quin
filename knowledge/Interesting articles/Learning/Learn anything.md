# Learn anything  
  
When you learn something, you need to set a clear goal, and you can do that by answering two questions:  
1. What will I be able to do that I can't do now?  
2. How will I prove it?  
  
The reason behind it is that if you create a vague goal, like "I want to learn economics or Python," your brain cannot comprehend it. Instead, say, "I am learning Python to build a web scraper."   
  
When you search for courses or sources to study, it is easy to become overwhelmed. A lot of people spend hours finding the best course on that topic. Instead, what is better is just to time box it for one hour.  
  
For example, you should search on the internet for a course, but it's also good to go to Reddit, YouTube, and Quora for people that give you a curated list of sources. Also make sure that you plan ahead and that you check, "Okay, what do I want in the future?"   
  
Make sure that you plan and schedule your sources that you're going to study so you will be able to track your progress. Add checkpoints where you answer yourself questions if you know it.  
  
The fourth principle is to make sure you're able to explain it according to the Richard Feynman method. His method is actually quite simple. If you cannot explain a topic simply, you do not properly understand it.   
  
Instead of going through the notes every week and reading them again, it's better to formulate five questions that you should answer yourself and test yourself and try to recall the notes from your memory. Research shows that when your memory struggles remembering something, it actually is much more strengthened in remembering it   
  
One other thing is, do not passively watch videos and read but also create new things from what you learned. Besides doing the notes, it's also important that you make sure that you are building something because that's the most important thing and makes you retain things
