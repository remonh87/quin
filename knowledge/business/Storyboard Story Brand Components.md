Notes from Donald Miller's StoryBrand framework — transcribed from a handwritten sketch.

## 1. The customer is the hero, not the brand

The idea is to become known for a certain thing and be believed at it well. Customers who consider doing business with you want to know:

- Can this brand really help me?
- How much does it cost?
- Where do I get it?
- How soon can I stop being at risk / get results?

**Story gap** — identifying a potential desire your customer has opens a story gap. A story gap grasps the customer's attention; if you don't find one, there's no reason for the customer to engage with us.

Be careful not to open too many story gaps — limit to a single focus. Humans can't hold large amounts of new information in their brains at once. Divide messaging like this:

```
Company
└── Division
    ├── Story Brand → Product
    └── Story Brand → Product
```

At the **company** level, the challenge is to define something simple and believable that customers can buy.

Another common problem: businesses focus on things unrelated to the customer's survival. If you're not offering something that connects to what customers need for their survival, you're leaving a story gap unopened.

**Human survival drivers:** be strong, healthy, safe, and happy.

Don't forget: customers want to remain safe. You are the safest place they'll ever be — unless you take that away, in which case they'll doubt and stop following you.

Don't assume customers understand what you're offering — tell them in plain, simple language.

## 2. Companies sell solutions to external problems, but customers buy to resolve internal problems

If there's no conflict in the story, there's no story gap — so it's important to be clear about the problems your customers face and how you solve them.

**Villain** — StoryBrand uses a villain to give conflict a clear point of focus. Four characteristics of a good villain:

1. Root source (e.g. carbs aren't the villain — but a broken metabolism is)
2. Relatable — recognized with disdain
3. Singular — for clarity
4. Real — don't be afraid of a bit of humor

**Three levels of conflict:**

- **External** — most businesses solve external conflicts (a leaky faucet, etc.) — this is often what customers think they're buying. The purpose of solving the external problem is to resolve the internal one.
- **Internal** — the key to discovering it is asking how the external problem makes the customer *feel* (tired, helpless, hopeless).
- **Philosophical** — creates depth and meaning; makes customers understand why the brand matters. Strong brands give customers a narrative of value beyond the product — a broader sense of purpose behind the decision.

The goal is to resolve the hero's external, internal, and philosophical conflict to earn the sale.

The product should solve a problem, evoke a feeling, and — where possible — contribute to something bigger (social justice, purpose, etc).

The "buy now" button should be framed as the action the customer takes to close the story gap.

## 3. Customers are looking for a guide, not another hero

A guide is someone who steps into our lives and helps us face challenges so we can overcome them and remain heroes ourselves.

The hero is capable but conflicted, and can't solve the problem alone — so they need a guide.

**Mistake:** many companies position themselves as the hero. Instead, position the *customer* as the hero and yourself as the guide.

The guide is a strong character in the story — but not the main character. The guide must be confident, otherwise they're useless.

**A guide serves the business through two characteristics:**

1. **Empathy** — "I understand, I care"
2. **Competence** — "I am qualified to help you"

- Empathy → people need to identify with you and your values.
- Competence → you don't need to be perfect, but you need to be confident and capable.

**How to show competence without bragging:**

1. Testimonials
2. Statistics (known metrics, user counts, etc.)
3. Awards
4. Press mentions
5. Logos

Don't brag — it's just to tick the box: "can I trust you?"

**First impression the customer needs answered:**

1. Can I trust you?
2. Can you help me?

Only after trust is established do people want to know you better.

## 4. Customers trust a guide who gives them a plan in baby steps

Once you've positioned yourself as the guide, customers want to know more and are hopeful you can help them. When they place an order, they're trusting you and putting skin in the game.

Often a customer hesitates to place an order because they don't know what working with the guide looks like.

```
Customer forms objections ← cognitive dissonance
  → identify baby steps to bridge the gap → plan
```

- Clarify what working with the business looks like
- Remove the sense of risk
